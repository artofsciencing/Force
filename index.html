⚡ Foundation
What is a Force?
A force is a push or a pull. Forces can cause objects to start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction. They can also change the shape of an object.

Forces are measured in Newtons (N), named after Sir Isaac Newton. We use an instrument called a Newton meter (or force meter) to measure them.

Forces are vectors — they have both a size (magnitude) and a direction.
Key Points
  • Forces are pushes or pulls
  • Unit of force = Newton (N)
  • Forces can change speed, direction, or shape
  • Measured with a Newton meter / force meter
  • Forces are vectors (magnitude + direction)
💡 Did You Know?
The weight of an average apple is approximately 1 Newton. That's why Newton used apples in his famous thought experiments about gravity!
🤝 Contact Forces
Contact Forces
Contact forces only act when two objects are physically touching. There are several types you need to know:
Friction Normal Reaction Air Resistance Thrust Tension Upthrust
Contact Force Types
  • Friction — opposes motion between surfaces in contact
  • Normal Reaction — surface pushes back at 90° to the surface
  • Air Resistance (Drag) — opposes motion through air
  • Thrust — driving force from an engine
  • Tension — force in a stretched rope or spring
  • Upthrust — upward force from a fluid (liquid or gas)
💡 Did You Know?
Friction can be both useful (brake pads, shoe soles) and a nuisance (inside bike gears). Engineers design lubricants to reduce unwanted friction.
🌍 Non-Contact Forces
Non-Contact Forces
Non-contact forces act between objects that are not physically touching. They act across a distance through fields.
Gravity Magnetism Electrostatic
Non-Contact Force Types
  • Gravitational force — acts between any two masses; always attracts, never repels
  • Magnetic force — between magnets or magnetic materials; can attract OR repel
  • Electrostatic force — between charged objects; can attract OR repel
💡 Did You Know?
Gravity is the weakest of the four fundamental forces — yet it holds galaxies together! The Earth's gravity keeps the Moon orbiting 384,000 km away.
⚖️ Balanced Forces
Balanced Forces
Forces are balanced when all the forces acting on an object cancel each other out — the resultant force is zero.

When forces are balanced, an object will: remain stationary (if already at rest) OR continue at constant velocity (if already moving). This is Newton's First Law of Motion.
Key Points — Balanced Forces
  • Resultant force = 0 N when balanced
  • Stationary object stays stationary
  • Moving object continues at same speed and direction
  • This is Newton's First Law of Motion
  • Example: a book on a table — weight balanced by normal reaction
🚀 Resultant Force
Resultant Force & Unbalanced Forces
The resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all the individual forces combined.

When forces are unbalanced (resultant ≠ 0), the object will accelerate in the direction of the resultant force. This leads to Newton's Second Law: F = m × a
Calculating Resultant Force
  • Forces in the same direction: add them together
  • Forces in opposite directions: subtract the smaller from the larger
  • Resultant ≠ 0 → object accelerates (Newton's 2nd Law)
  • F = m × a (force in N, mass in kg, acceleration in m/s²)
  • Direction of acceleration = direction of resultant force
💡 Did You Know?
A skydiver reaches terminal velocity when air resistance equals their weight — the resultant force becomes zero, so they stop accelerating!
